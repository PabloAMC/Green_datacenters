Off-Grid Datacenter LCOE

The least-cost mix of solar, wind, battery and gas backup to run an always-on, off-grid datacenter on mostly-renewable power — and the year going renewable beats burning gas, across the US and Europe.

▶  View the source code & full methodology on GitHub

Read this first. This is a stylised techno-economic model: trust the directional comparisons, not absolute numbers to better than ~±20–30%. The headline runs on synthetic (not measured) weather and a single generation site by default; both can be replaced with real reanalysis and multi-site portfolios. All numbers below are generated directly from the model's exported results.

Key findings

  • Europe — renewables already win at moderate shares. Carbon-priced, expensive EU gas (rising from $114 to $163/MWh) means a firm 70%-renewable build reaches parity ~2025, and even a 90%-renewable one by ~2029.
  • US — cheap gas is a moat at high renewable shares. Untaxed ~$46/MWh gas keeps a 90%-renewable build off parity beyond 2040 (US 90% crossover >2040); only moderate-share builds cross, in the mid-to-late 2030s.
  • A firm battery-only system tops out near ~94% renewable. Multi-day Dunkelflaute neither sun nor wind covers, and battery power can't bridge days — the last few percent always fall to gas. Going higher needs long-duration storage or hydrogen.
  • A fully gas-free, zero-carbon datacenter is feasible. A co-optimised solar+wind+battery+green-hydrogen build delivers $148→$85/MWh in the EU, crossing below gas around 2029.
  • Where you build dominates the cost. Across a poor→good site in a region the 90%-renewable delivered cost spans tens of $/MWh (the shaded bands in the figures); a multi-site portfolio softens the multi-day lulls and cuts high-renewable cost materially.
  • Off-grid is itself a premium. Staying on the grid with a renewable-energy contract sits below the off-grid high-renewable optimum in both regions — off-grid buys siting independence, at a cost.

What is and isn't modelled

  • Default = firm, always-on: gas backup covers 100% of load during lulls, so the worst case is a known, capped fuel cost. Premium/AI workloads never shed and collapse to this case.
  • Weather is synthetic but structured (multi-day Dunkelflaute, region-specific resource and sun–wind correlation). A real-weather seam (--weather, ERA5/NSRDB) and a multi-site portfolio (--sites) are built in but opt-in.
  • Single site by default — the largest directional caveat; a geographic portfolio softens the tails and lowers high-renewable cost.
  • Not modelled: sub-hourly load variation, on-site fuel logistics, transmission.
